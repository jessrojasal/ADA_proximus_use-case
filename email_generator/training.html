Phishing Prevention Guide

Best Practices for next time

  • Verify Email Senders
    • Check the Email Domain and Sender Address:
      • Ensure the sender’s email address matches the official domain (e.g., @company.com vs. @company.co).
      • Be wary of addresses with slight misspellings, extra numbers, or letters (e.g., @micr0soft.com vs. @microsoft.com).
    • Look for Generic Greetings:
      • Legitimate companies often address you by name. Be cautious of emails starting with “Dear Customer” or “Dear Sir/Madam.”
    • Inspect Email Headers:
      • Email headers reveal technical details about the sender. Contact IT if unsure how to interpret them.
  • Recognize Phishing Signs
    • Urgent or Threatening Language:
      • Watch out for phrases like "Your account will be locked in 24 hours!"
      • Scammers use urgency to pressure you into acting without thinking.
    • Poor Grammar and Formatting:
      • Legitimate organizations usually proofread their emails. Misspellings, unusual capitalization, or awkward formatting are red flags.
    • Suspicious Attachments:
      • Avoid downloading unsolicited attachments, especially those with extensions like .exe, .zip, .rar, .docm, or .xlsm.
  • Secure Your Credentials
    • Never Share Passwords:
      • Legitimate companies will never ask for your password via email, phone, or text.
    • Use a Password Manager:
      • A password manager stores complex passwords securely, reducing the risk of reusing passwords.
    • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA):
      • Use 2FA whenever available to add an extra layer of security to your accounts.
  • Beware of Fake Websites
    • Check for HTTPS:
      • Ensure the URL starts with https://. A padlock icon in the address bar indicates a secure connection.
    • Scrutinize the URL:
      • Beware of subtle misspellings or added characters in the domain (e.g., www.paypai.com instead of www.paypal.com).
    • Avoid Public Wi-Fi for Sensitive Transactions:
      • Use a Virtual Private Network (VPN) to secure your internet connection on public Wi-Fi.
  • Report Suspicious Emails
    • Forward Emails to IT:
      • If you suspect an email is phishing, forward it to your IT security team or designated email address (e.g., phishing@company.com).
    • Avoid Clicking Links:
      • Do not interact with links or attachments in suspicious emails, even if they seem urgent.
    • Use Reporting Tools:
      • Many email platforms (e.g., Gmail, Outlook) have built-in options to report phishing attempts.
  • Additional Preventative Measures
    • Monitor Financial Accounts:
      • Regularly check bank and credit card statements for unauthorized transactions. Set up account alerts if available.
    • Install Anti-Malware Software:
      • Use reputable antivirus and anti-malware programs to detect and block malicious software.
    • Keep Software Updated:
      • Regularly update your operating system, browser, and apps to patch security vulnerabilities.
    • Verify Unexpected Communications:
      • Confirm the legitimacy of unusual requests from coworkers or vendors through official channels before responding.
  • Training and Awareness
    • Participate in Phishing Simulations:
      • Use mock phishing campaigns to practice identifying threats.
    • Attend Cybersecurity Workshops:
      • Stay updated on the latest phishing tactics through regular training sessions.
    • Share Knowledge:
      • Encourage coworkers to report phishing attempts and share tips to stay safe.
